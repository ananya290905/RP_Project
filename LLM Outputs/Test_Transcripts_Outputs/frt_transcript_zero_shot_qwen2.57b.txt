Context : This is a deliberative transcript discussing the implementation of facial recognition technology in public spaces.

Commander Walsh: I’ll go. I have been working in the police for over two decades now. We lost three officers last year to incidents that could have been anticipated. When I look at what facial recognition can do, I see something that keeps people safe. We deployed it at the transit hub last year on a trial basis and flagged four individuals with warrants for violent offenses in a single weekend, it was highly effective.

Priya Nair : I understand why that sounds compelling but the question isn’t whether it works sometimes, the question is what will be the implications if we normalize this. The National Institute of Standards and Technology found error rates up to a hundred times higher for Black and Asian faces compared to white faces. So when you say it flags suspects, is it doing so without bias?

John Smith : That is exactly what I see in my community. People in Rotterdam are already feeling like they are being watched. My neighbour is in his sixties and was stopped three times last year. Not because he did anything, but because the system got it ‘wrong’.

Tobias Ehrlich : If I can add some context here, the EU AI Act treats real time remote biometric identification in public spaces as one of the highest risk categories of AI use.

Councillor Mwangi : I understand how FRT carries a lot of risk but time and time again I see my friends come to me in tears because their car was broken into for the third time, or their child was mugged outside a school. I am not willing to just say not to tools that might prevent that.

Eric Calloway : I think both things can be true at once, can’t they? While trust matters enormously, peoples’ behaviour can change when they are being surveilled and not in a good way.

Commander Walsh: But we already have CCTV everywhere, nobody is protesting that. Why is facial recognition technology categorically different?

Priya Nair : Because CCTV is passive, you review footage after something happens. FRT is active, it identifies you in real time without your knowledge or consent and before anything has even happened. You are not just affecting privacy, you are showing that you do not trust people and cutting out free expression.
John Smith : Yes, that is exactly it. I have had people in my community say that they won’t come to our neighbourhood meetings anymore because they walk past council cameras on the way. Imagine if those cameras knew their name.

Tobias Ehrlich: There is also a question of proportionality here. Human Right Watch have called a ban on this precisely because they argue no use case can  justify the risk at a population level.

Councillor Mwangi : But those are advocacy organisations, the police also have an obligation to the public they serve. I am not saying we give this technology unlimited power, I agree there needs to be safeguards but I am not ready to say never.

Commander Walsh : That is my position too, honestly, I am not asking for a free pass, I just want regulated use. Warrants for targeting individuals, independent oversight, regular auditing of accuracy data, I am all for that. If the error rates for certain demographics are higher, that has to be addressed before deployment, not after.

Eric Calloway : That is something I can work with too. Though I want to know who oversees the oversight, that does not seem straightforward.

Priya Nair : The difficulty is that once this infrastructure exists, it expands. You start with watch lists for violent offenders and five years later you are using it at protests, it just doesn’t stay in its box.

Commander Walsh: I will say this, the current situation isn’t ideal either. Not having tools means crimes go unsolved, that is also a cost and it is not evenly distributed. It is the people in the most deprived neighbourhoods who bear that cost when policing is under-resourced.

Priya Nair : That is a fair point. I don’t think under-resourcing is solved by surveillance though but rather an investment.

Eric Calloway : Can I ask something though, is there evidence that crime gets reduced long term in cities which use this technology?

Commander Walsh: Honestly, it is mixed. The evidence base is still developing which is part of why I am advocating for regulated pilots rather than either a permanent rollout or permanent ban.
