Director Font : I will be direct with the intentions of this, we retain biometric data collected at entry points for five years. This will be searchable by border agencies and under a warrant by domestic law enforcement.

Amara Diea: I want to challenge this. The people whose data we are talking about are not a homogeneous group. You have tourists, students, refugees coming in and treating all of them as a potential security risk by default and holding their data is not a neutral administrative decision.

Dr. Hoof : Can I ask what the actual security justification is for five years specifically? Because the case I have seen tends to centre on repeat entry patterns and watch list matching, you can do that with a much smaller window.

Director Font : Investigations take time, you might not know someone is relevant to an inquiry until two or three years after they entered. Without retained data, we would not have been able to reconstruct the network for cases that last much longer.

Councillor Lemaire : That is a real case and I do not want to minimize it. But I think it is also the kind of exceptional case that tends to drive policy in ways that affect millions or ordinary people who never become relevant to any investigation. We are essentially building infrastructure around the worst case.

Amara Diea: And it is worth saying who bears the cost of that infrastructure disproportionately. The database might not treat everyone the same even if the policy says it does.

Director Font: That is an implementation question not a retention question. If there is bias in how the data is searched that should be addressed separately.

Amara Diea: I don’t think you can separate them that cleanly. The existence of the database enables the bias. You cannot misuse data that doesn’t exist.

Dr. Hoof : There is also a question of what happens when this data is breached. Biometric data is permanently tied to a person, so the risk calculus for storing it at scale is genuinely different from other categories of personal data.

Director Font: Every system carries breach risk. The question is whether the security benefit outweighs it and for me, it does.

Councillor Lemaire : I think most of us would accept that some retention is justified. The disagreement is about duration, scope and access. Five years searchable by law enforcement feels like a significant step beyond what is required.

Dr. Hoof : I agree with that. If the purpose is border security then border agencies having access during the period of a person’s visa or leave is defensible. Domestic law enforcement access under warrant only is a different proposition to what’s on the table.

Fatima Rashid : There are people in this room who are talking about refugees and asylum seekers as though retention of their data is equivalent to retention of a tourist's data. It is not. For someone who has fled an authoritarian government, having their biometric data in a state database that could be accessed, potentially shared with other states, is not an abstract concern. It is a life or death question for some people. 

Amara Diea: Yes, exactly. And the data sharing agreements we currently have with countries are not public, so we do not actually know where this data ends up.

Director Font : Data sharing is governed by strict bilateral agreements and legal frameworks. I understand the concern but it is not as if we are sending this to anyone who asks.

Fatima Rashid : With respect, the legal frameworks do not always hold. There have been cases of data being shared outside agreed channels. I do not think citing the existence of a framework is the same as guaranteeing it works.

Councillor Lemaire: I think that is a fair point and it is one of the reasons I would want any expanded retention to come with an independent audit mechanism. 

Director Font : I am not opposed to oversight in principle. My concern is that every additional layer of process slows down the use of the data when speed matters.

Dr. Hoof : That tension between oversights and speed is valid but I still don’t think it resolves in favour of less oversight just because it is inconvenient.

Fatima Rashid : Can I ask whether there has been any consultation with the communities most affected by this?

Amara Diea: I agree, if we are deciding what is acceptable and what is not, we should consult the people who are most affected.
