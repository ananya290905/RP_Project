Context : This is a deliberative transcript discussing the use of police drones equipped with cameras and microphones at large public events.

Chief Novak : We used drones at three events last year, a festival, a march and the cup final. They were useful, you get situational awareness you simply cannot get from ground level. We could spot crown crush development and redirect officers before it became serious.

Yasmine Osei : I want to take that seriously because safety is a huge concern but I think that there is something uncomfortable about using it at marches where people have a right to attend without being recorded from above by police.

Chief Novak : I understand your concern but we are not targeting individuals, the drone was monitoring crowd density.

Yasmic Osei : But that footage still exists right? And the people at the march didn't know they were being filmed. What happened to the footage after? Did it get deleted? Who has access?

Marcus Trelawney : I agree, if people know or suspect that police drones are recording protests, some of them won’t come. That is a cost to political participation.

Chief Novak : I understand that concern but I would push back slightly on your framing. We do not publish the fact that we use drones but it isn’t hidden either, you can find it in the local press.

Marcus Trelawney : Being in the local press is not the same as informed consent though.

Dr. Mulligan : I would say that the legal bar for justifying that interference is high and the justification needs to be documented and proportionate to a specific purpose.

Chief Novak : We do document everything, every deployment has a log.

Dr. Mulligan : Is that log reviewable anywhere? By an independent body?

Chief Novak : No, not by an independent body but we do have internal reviews by the Police and Crime Commissioner’s office.

Marcus Trelawney : I think that the independence question is quite important here. None of us in this room are saying the police should have zero surveillance capability. The question is who decides when it is appropriate and who checks that.

Chief Novak : That is a reasonable question. I do not want unchecked authority either, I just need the decision making process not to be so slow that it is useless in a fast moving situation.

Dr. Mulligan : Those two things are not necessarily in conflict. You can have a system with pre-authorisation frameworks where certain event types or conditions are pre-approved.

Yasmin Osei : I think that is worth exploring. I would also want the pre-authorisation criteria to exclude political demonstrations though unless there is sign of a threat. 

Chief Novak : That is something I could work with I think.

Dr. Mulligan : There is also the audio question which we haven’t touched on yet. The drones you’re using have audio capability?

Chief Novak : They do have it but it isn’t really something we routinely activate.

Dr. Mulligan : Okay but there is still a possibility of activating it? Audio recording in a public space raises a lot of questions for me.

Yasmin Osei : I think regardless of where we land on the rest of this, audio should be off by default and require separate authorisation. That seems reasonable to me.

Marcus Trelawney : Yes, no audio should be the bare minimum.

Chief Novak : We usually take legal advice before any audio use to be clear but I do not have a strong objection to excluding audio as a default position.

Dr. Mulligan : I agree, audio should be out of the question, it is unacceptable to be recording people’s voices. I think it might also be worth it to talk to civil society groups especially when it comes to using these drones at protests or marches.

Yasmin Osei : I agree, I do not think the four of us should be deciding the rules for surveilling political events and gatherings. 

Marcus Trelawney : I also agree with that.
