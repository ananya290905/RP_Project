Context : This is a deliberative transcript discussing the implementation of algorithmic threat scoring.

Prof. Okoro : We’re discussing algorithmic threat scoring today, which are systems that assign individuals a numerical risk score to predict likelihood of involvement in violence, either as perpetrator or victim.

Detective Bright : I want to be direct about what these tools are and aren’t in practice. They are not replacing officer judgement, they are triage tools, helping us prioritise when we’re under-resourced. We have a city with two hundred and eighty violent incidents last year and a team that cannot investigate all of them with equal depth. The algorithm will tell us where to look first.

Dr. Hassan : That framing matters a great deal though because Chicago’s Strategic Subject List started with that same premise and ended up with over four hundred thousand people assigned risk scores. Fifty-six percent of black men in the city aged twenty to twenty-nine were on the list.

Leona Washington : I want to say something before we get too deep into framing. My cousin was on a list like this, he has been a victim of robbery and because the algorithm weights prior contact with the system, including as a victim, his score went up. He started getting visits, his employer found out and he lost his job. 

Erik Lindqvist : As someone who worked on these systems before, the variables that go into a risk score sound neutral, like age, prior arrests, network ties, but they are not neutral. They are proxies for race and poverty, you put biased data in and you get biased scores out, no matter how sophisticated the math is.

Councillor Bridges : I hear all of this and I do take it seriously but I also have a responsibility to people who are experiencing violence right now. Last month a teenager was stabbed two streets down from where I live. If there was a tool that could have predicted that with any reliability and we didn’t use it, how do I justify that? I am not dismissing bias concerns but saying that the alternative of doing nothing also has a cost.

Frida Diallo : These scores are being used to inform bail decisions, parole recommendations and  in some cases sentencing. A black-box algorithm producing a number that affects whether someone goes to prison is a due process problem. 

Detective Bright : We don’t use it in sentencing or to arrest people.

Dr. Hassan : It matters operationally. If high-scoring individuals receive more police attention, they accumulate more system contact, which raises their score, which generates more attention. The algorithm reinforces the pattern it is supposed to predict. Chicago’s own data showed predictions were wrong more than ninety-nine times out of a hundred in terms of crime location.

Leona Washington : And the communities that bear the cost of that noise are also not random. It is always the same community.

Erik Lindqvist : What I would say is that fairness is not a single thing. You can build an algorithm that is statistically fair by one measure and systematically unfair by another measure. You have to choose and that choice is a value judgement, not a technical one. 

Councillor Bridges : So what do we do instead? Because the answer can’t just be don’t do anything.

Frida Diallo : Community investment. Structural intervention. There is strong evidence that interrupting the conditions that produce violence like education failure or unemployment reduced violence more durably than surveillance. 

Detective Bright : I agree with that in principle but those are long term solutions. I am responsible for what happens next week. The framing of this as surveillance vs. investment frustrates me because the people arguing for investment aren’t the ones who have to explain to a family why we didn’t act on information we had.

Dr. Hassan  : That cuts the other way too though. The people arguing for surveillance aren’t the ones who have to explain to a family why their father lost his job because an algorithm flagged him.

Leona Washington : I don’t think anyone is saying let violence happen. The disagreement is about whether this tool prevents violence or just redistributes its costs onto communities that are already bearing more than their share.

Councillor Bridges : I can work with that. If the evidence shows the tool doesn’t actually reduce violence then the argument for it is really just about the appearance of doing something.

Erik Lindqvist : I’d add, open source the methodology. If you can’t publish the algorithm because it would be gamed, that is a sign the algorithm is the wrong tool for a public governance function.
